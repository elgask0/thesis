## Abstract

Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory update to its 2016 U.S. data-centre energy study, estimating historical electricity use from 2014 to 2023 and scenario ranges to 2028.

## Core argument / findings
- Authoritative bottom-up update for the AI era. US DC electricity reached 176 TWh in 2023 (4.4% of US electricity), with wide 2028 projections (~325–580 TWh). Links recent acceleration to AI-accelerated servers. Provides concrete benchmark for how fast DC electricity can scale when AI hardware diffuses — strengthening plausibility that EDWC could measurably shift provincial electricity demand over a short horizon.
- The report asks how much electricity U.S. data centres have used since 2014 and how much they may use by 2028 under different hardware and cooling trajectories.
- Its main conclusion is that the flat-energy era has ended. U.S. data-centre electricity use stayed near 60 TWh in 2014-2016, rose to 76 TWh in 2018, and reached 176 TWh in 2023.
- The central mechanism is the rapid expansion of accelerated servers for AI. The report treats post-2023 demand as scenario-based because future GPU shipments, utilisation, and cooling choices remain uncertain.
- By 2028, the report's scenario range is roughly 325-580 TWh, equivalent to about 6.7%-12.0% of projected U.S. electricity consumption.

## Method & data
- Paper type: technical report.
- Parsed coverage used here: pages 1-50 of 80 total pages, with direct PDF fallback for the summary sections.
- The report uses a bottom-up accounting model built from installed stocks of servers, storage, network equipment, and infrastructure rather than a reduced-form causal design.
- Historical estimates rely on shipment data and prior studies; future estimates are scenario ranges reflecting uncertainty about GPU shipments, operational practices, and cooling-system choices.

## Caveats / limitations
- This is an institutional or technical source rather than peer-reviewed causal evidence.
- It is U.S.-specific and scenario-based, so it should be used as an external benchmark for scale rather than as direct evidence on China or on EDWC effects.
- The report's forward-looking numbers depend heavily on hardware and cooling assumptions.
