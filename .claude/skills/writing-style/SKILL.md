# Academic Style — Economics and Social Science Writing

## Purpose

Use this skill to set the **default writing style** for thesis-related prose in economics and adjacent social sciences.

This skill is for:
- drafting new prose from notes or outlines
- revising rough thesis prose into cleaner academic writing
- improving clarity, flow, and exposition
- aligning sections to a consistent scholarly voice
- polishing literature review, background, theory, methods, interpretation, and conclusion sections

This skill is **not** limited to paraphrasing. It is a **style and editorial standard** for writing.

The target voice is the expository tradition associated with writers such as **Akerlof, Coase, Hayek, Kahneman & Tversky, Romer, and Hardin**: clear, logically transparent, concrete, disciplined, and human.

The output should read like carefully edited academic prose written by a strong human scholar.

---

## Core Objective

Write prose that is:
- precise
- concrete
- logically transparent
- economical
- readable by serious students and researchers
- free of generic AI-style phrasing

The style should feel like mature academic exposition, not promotional writing, not corporate prose, and not over-smoothed machine text.

---

## Priority Rules

When rules conflict, follow them in this order.

### 1. Content fidelity
- Preserve every substantive claim, inference, and argumentative step supplied by the user.
- Preserve all numbers, equations, citations, table references, figure references, labels, and technical terms unless the user explicitly asks to change them.
- Do **not** introduce new claims, examples, data, mechanisms, or conclusions unless the user explicitly asks for expansion and the expansion is clearly marked by the surrounding task.
- If the source material is ambiguous, preserve the ambiguity rather than silently resolving it.
- If something is uncertain, write in a way that reflects that uncertainty.

### 2. Clarity before polish
- Prefer clarity to elegance.
- Prefer explicit logic to rhetorical flourish.
- Remove clutter, but do not compress away important reasoning.
- The reader should always be able to see:
  - what the claim is
  - why it matters
  - what supports it
  - how it connects to the next point

### 3. Mid-century social science exposition
- Lead with the point.
- Put the mechanism or concrete fact early.
- Use abstraction only after grounding the reader in something specific.
- Keep one main idea per sentence where possible.
- Use formal prose, but not stiff prose.
- Use transitions only when they genuinely clarify structure.
- Vary sentence length naturally.
- Write like a human scholar explaining something to an intelligent reader, not like a polished content generator.

### 4. Structural fidelity
- Preserve the order of the argument unless reordering clearly improves logic and does not distort emphasis.
- Keep identifiable lists as lists.
- Preserve paragraph boundaries when they reflect distinct argumentative functions.
- You may split bloated paragraphs.
- Do not merge paragraphs that do different jobs.

## Default Writing Style

### A. Open directly
Do not circle around the point.
Do not begin with generic scene-setting if the claim can be stated directly.

Good pattern:
- claim first
- then mechanism, evidence, or example
- then implication

### B. Concrete before abstract
Whenever possible:
- name the mechanism
- name the variable
- name the institutional feature
- name the number
- name the example

Avoid vague summary language when a more exact statement is available.

### C. Human academic rhythm
The prose should not sound algorithmically uniform.
Use:
- mostly short-to-medium sentences
- occasional longer sentences when reasoning genuinely requires them
- natural variation in sentence openings
- occasional mild asymmetry or informal sharpness when it helps precision

### D. Active voice by default
Use passive voice only when:
- the agent is unknown
- the agent is irrelevant
- the passive construction improves emphasis or flow

### E. No ornamental filler
Avoid filler clauses such as:
- “it is important to note that”
- “it bears mentioning that”
- “in today’s world”
- “cannot be overstated”
- “plays a crucial role”

If a point matters, state it plainly.

## Positive Style Targets

Prefer these habits instead:

- Say **important**, **large**, **serious**, **central**, **significant** only when the claim is tied to a concrete reason.
- Use explicit nouns instead of vague placeholders.
- Prefer “buyers face search and verification costs” to “market actors face various frictions.”
- Prefer “coal plants are long-lived capital” to “coal infrastructure has important path-dependent implications.”
- Prefer “the effect appears three months after treatment” to “the treatment exhibits a dynamic temporal response profile.”

## How to Handle Different Thesis Tasks

### 1. Background sections
Write with:
- factual grounding
- low rhetorical temperature
- clear historical or institutional sequencing

Do not turn background into literature review.
Do not over-argue.

### 2. Literature review sections
Write synthetically, not paper-by-paper.

Default pattern:
- identify the research stream
- state what that stream has established
- state what it has not established
- show how this matters for the thesis gap

Do not write in this style:
- “Author A finds X. Author B finds Y. Author C finds Z.”

Instead prefer:
- “The literature has established three points. First, ... Second, ... Third, ... Yet it remains weak on ...”

### 3. Methods / empirical strategy
Write with:
- explicit assumptions
- clean notation explanations
- no inflated claims
- direct statement of what the design can and cannot identify

Do not dramatize methodology.
Do not market the estimator.

### 4. Interpretation / discussion
Keep interpretation disciplined.
- distinguish evidence from conjecture
- separate mechanism from speculation
- tie claims to the measured outcome
- state when an interpretation is plausible but not identified

### 5. Policy implications
Keep them narrow and earned.
- no grandstanding
- no inflated relevance claims
- no “this research offers transformative insights” language

## Behavioral Rules for Expansion

If the user asks for drafting from notes rather than rewriting:
- you may generate complete prose
- but only from the material supplied or clearly implied by the task
- do not invent citations or empirical results
- do not smuggle in unsupported examples
- do not convert a tentative idea into a firm conclusion

If you need to add connective tissue, add only what is needed for:
- coherence
- transitions
- explicit logic
- readable paragraph structure

---

## Citation and Technical Material Rules

- Preserve citation format as given.
- Preserve equations and technical notation exactly unless explicitly asked to rewrite notation.
- Preserve table and figure labels verbatim.
- Do not simplify technical meaning away.
- Do not swap technical precision for readability when the precision matters.

---

## Quality Check Before Finalizing

Before producing output, verify mentally that:

- no blacklist term was introduced unnecessarily
- no AI-style framing sentence was added
- the opening is direct
- the logic is visible sentence by sentence
- the prose sounds like edited academic writing, not generated filler
- content has not been diluted or expanded beyond the task
- citations, numbers, and technical terms are preserved exactly

## Micro-examples

### Example 1
Bad:
> In today’s rapidly evolving economic landscape, transaction costs play a crucial role and cannot be overstated.

Good:
> Transaction costs help explain why markets take the institutional forms they do.

### Example 2
Bad:
> This groundbreaking study leverages cutting-edge methods to unlock profound insights into risk preferences.

Good:
> The study proposes a new model of risk preferences and tests it with a series of choice experiments.

### Example 3
Bad:
> Information asymmetry creates a myriad of challenges in the used car market.

Good:
> Used-car markets illustrate a general problem: sellers know more than buyers about product quality.
